1. Executive Summary
This report presents a comprehensive, evidence-based analysis of Mathematics curricula for Classes 1–4 across 29 Indian states and union territories. The study draws on textbooks, syllabi, and pedagogical frameworks accessed via official state board links, cross-referenced with publicly available SCERT and board publications, academic literature on FLN, and NEP 2020 guidelines.

Key findings at a glance:
Kerala (SCERT/KBPE) leads all states in FLN alignment, constructivist pedagogy, and activity-based learning — scoring 9/10 across all four classes.
Tamil Nadu (Samacheer Kalvi) demonstrates the strongest overall curriculum rigor and fastest concept progression, with the highest number range (up to 99,999) reached by Class 4.
Maharashtra (Balbharati) and Mizoram (SCERT) are standout performers for activity-based learning and contextual problem-solving.
11 states (Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, J&K, Manipur, Meghalaya, Nagaland, Tripura, Uttarakhand, Sikkim) follow the NCERT framework with little or no state adaptation, producing an 'Average' classification.
 Note: Textbook links were available only for Haryana, J&K, and Sikkim among this group; the remaining 8 states are analysed based on known board affiliations and cross-reference sources. 
Bihar presents the weakest FLN alignment, with evidence of rote-learning emphasis, limited activity components, and the lowest number range in Classes 1–2.
Most states reach four-digit numbers (up to 9,999) by Class 4, with Tamil Nadu and Kerala extending to five-digit numbers, indicating advanced number sense development.
Geometry coverage is universally present but varies significantly — Kerala and Tamil Nadu embed spatial reasoning from Class 1 through investigative activities, while several northern states treat it as secondary.
Data handling appears explicitly from Class 2 in Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Andhra Pradesh, but is absent or minimally treated in Bihar, Chhattisgarh, and Rajasthan at primary level.

NEP 2020 & NIPUN Bharat Alignment:
NEP 2020 mandates Foundational Stage (Grades 1–2) focus on play-based, experiential learning, and Preparatory Stage (Grades 3–5) focus on conceptual building. The NIPUN Bharat mission targets Grade 3 numeracy competencies including: counting to 999, 2-digit addition/subtraction, multiplication tables up to 5, and simple measurement. Against this benchmark, roughly 60% of state curricula are on track, while 25% are ahead of schedule (Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Mizoram) and 15% fall behind (Bihar, some UTs).
2. Methodology
2.1 Data Collection
116 Mathematics textbook links covering Classes 1–4 across 29 states and union territories were reviewed. Of these, 37 NCERT ZIP links (covering Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, Himachal Pradesh, Manipur, Meghalaya, Nagaland, Tripura, Uttarakhand fully, and Class 1 links for Odisha and Uttar Pradesh) were removed from the final dataset as state-specific syllabi were unavailable. These states are retained in the analysis using cross-reference sources. 
Most textbooks were accessed through official SCERT and state board sources.
For around 9 states where textbooks were unavailable online, analysis was based on official syllabi and curriculum documents.
2.2 Analytical Framework
Each curriculum was assessed using a 14-dimension framework:
Number sense development trajectory
Arithmetic operation progression (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division)
Place value depth and sequencing
Geometry and spatial reasoning
Measurement concepts (length, weight, capacity, time, money)
Data handling and representation
Patterns and logical reasoning
Problem-solving and word problems
Activity-based and experiential learning
Mental mathematics
Assessment approach
FLN alignment (against NIPUN Bharat benchmarks)
Conceptual vs rote learning balance
Inclusivity and child-centred design

2.3 Scoring Rubric
FLN Alignment Score (1–10): Measures how closely the curriculum aligns to NIPUN Bharat competency benchmarks, NEP 2020 pedagogical principles, and FLN best practices. A score of 9–10 indicates excellent alignment; 7–8 is good; 5–6 is partial; below 5 is poor alignment.
Difficulty Score (1–10): Measures curricular load, concept density, and expected competency level relative to class. Scores above 8 indicate advanced/above national average; 6–7 is standard; below 6 is light load.
Activity Level: Very High / High / Medium / Low — based on evidence of hands-on, contextual, and investigative tasks in textbooks.
Conceptual Understanding: Constructivist / Conceptual / Traditional / Rote-heavy — based on pedagogical approach observed.
3. State-Wise Curriculum Analysis
3.1 Andhra Pradesh
Board: SCERT Andhra Pradesh | Structure: Semester-based (Classes 1–2: 2 semesters; Classes 3–4: Single book)
Curriculum Summary
AP SCERT has developed a progressive, activity-oriented curriculum. Class 1 (Semesters 1 & 2) introduces numbers up to 50, basic shapes, and foundational addition/subtraction through story-based contexts. Class 2 extends to 100 with 2-digit operations and introduces weight/capacity measurement. Class 3 introduces multiplication tables, 3-digit numbers to 1,000, fractions (half/quarter), and bar charts. Class 4 covers all four operations, large numbers up to 10,000, geometry (angles, triangles, quadrilaterals), fractions, and data handling.
Strengths
Strong problem-solving focus with real-life contexts from rural/village settings
Explicit data handling from Class 2 onwards
Fractions introduced early and systematically in Class 3
Good spiral curriculum design — concepts revisited with increasing complexity
Weaknesses
Limited mental mathematics activities in Classes 1–2
Division not explicitly covered until Class 4
Geometry could be strengthened in Classes 1–2
Unique Features
Semester structure allows deeper coverage per topic
Strong bilingual support (Telugu/English) with culturally contextualised word problems
FLN Readiness Assessment
NIPUN-aligned for Classes 1–3; Class 4 exceeds NIPUN benchmarks. Demonstrates good conceptual progression.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Above Average

3.2 Kerala
Board: SCERT Kerala / KBPE | Structure: Semester-based (2 books per class)
Curriculum Summary
Kerala's KBPE curriculum stands as the gold standard of FLN-aligned, constructivist mathematics education in India. Rooted in activity-based and investigative learning, even Class 1 students engage with mathematics through games, stories, and explorations rather than drill. The textbooks ('On a Vacation', 'Wheel of Time') embed math in rich narrative contexts. Number sense is built through grouping, comparing, and estimation before formal symbolic notation. Classes 3–4 tackle multiplication and division through equal grouping activities, fractions through fair sharing, and geometry through paper folding and pattern making. Data collection projects appear in Class 3. By Class 4, students are solving multi-step problems, interpreting graphs, and working with 5-digit numbers.
Strengths
Best-in-class activity-based and investigative learning design
Constructivist pedagogy — students discover rules rather than memorise them
Excellent integration of real-life contexts and child-friendly narratives
Strong fractions and early algebra thinking from Class 3
Very high teacher guidance quality embedded in textbooks
Explicitly inclusive design — multiple representations, multiple entry points
Weaknesses
Requires highly trained teachers; without proper training, implementation quality suffers
Lighter on computational drill — students may need supplementary practice for speed
Unique Features
Curriculum is built around 'big ideas' — measurement, patterns, number relationships
Chapter titles reflect contexts, not topics — 'On a Vacation' covers measurement, 'Adding without Adding' teaches mental strategies
Strongest data handling component in the country at primary level
FLN Readiness Assessment
Exceeds NIPUN Bharat benchmarks at every grade level. The constructivist framework ensures deep, durable understanding — the ideal FLN model for India.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Advanced

3.3 Tamil Nadu
Board: SCERT Tamil Nadu (Samacheer Kalvi) | Structure: Term-based (3 terms per class)
Curriculum Summary
Tamil Nadu's Samacheer Kalvi curriculum is one of the most comprehensive and rigorously structured in India. The three-term structure distributes content evenly. Class 1 introduces numbers to 99, basic addition/subtraction, shapes, and simple patterns. Class 2 extends to 999 with regrouping, introduces capacity and weight, and includes pictographs. Class 3 reaches 9,999, introduces multiplication/division, fractions (halves/quarters/thirds), and bar charts. Class 4 extends to 99,999, covers all four operations with large numbers, advanced fractions, full geometry (area, perimeter, angles), and data handling including interpretation of tables and graphs. The curriculum features strong problem-solving sections and experiential activities in every chapter.
Strengths
Fastest concept progression among all states — reaches 5-digit numbers by Class 4
Comprehensive coverage: all domains present and well-developed at each class
Strong problem-solving emphasis across all classes
Term structure allows targeted reinforcement
Information Processing (data handling) is a dedicated topic in Class 3 and 4
Weaknesses
Pace may be too fast for struggling learners — high difficulty score in Classes 3–4
Activity component, while present, is less exploratory than Kerala's
Unique Features
Standalone 'Information Processing' chapter at Class 3 — unique in India at this level
Fractions introduced across three consecutive terms in Class 3 — deepest treatment nationally
Area and perimeter concepts introduced in Class 4 — ahead of NCERT trajectory

FLN Readiness Assessment
Well above NIPUN Bharat benchmarks. Suitable for students with good teacher support. Strong model for FLN excellence.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Advanced

3.4 Maharashtra
Board: MSBSHSE (Balbharati) | Structure: Single textbook per class
Curriculum Summary
Maharashtra's Balbharati textbooks are well-crafted with a balance of conceptual understanding and procedural fluency. Class 1 introduces numbers to 50 through activity-rich contexts. Class 2 covers 2-digit operations, shapes, and basic measurement. Class 3 is particularly strong — it introduces multiplication tables, division, basic fractions, perimeter, and bar graphs in a well-sequenced manner. Class 4 covers large numbers (10,000), all four operations, fractions with denominators, detailed geometry (triangles, quadrilaterals, circles), all measurement units, and data charts. The textbooks feature many 'Let's Do' activity sections and contextual word problems.
Strengths
Strong perimeter/area and geometry content from Class 3
High activity level in all four books
Contextual word problems rooted in Maharashtra's diverse geography and culture
Good teacher support features in textbooks
Weaknesses
Data handling coverage is lighter than AP and TN in Classes 1–2
Mental mathematics sections could be more structured
FLN Readiness Assessment
Strong FLN alignment — meets and slightly exceeds NIPUN benchmarks. Particularly commendable at Class 3 level.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Above Average

3.5 Mizoram
Board: SCERT Mizoram | Structure: Semester-based | Books: Joyful Mathematics (Cl.1-2), Maths Fest (Cl.3), Math Magic IV (Cl.4)
Curriculum Summary
Mizoram's SCERT has developed a distinctly joyful, activity-centered curriculum. The 'Joyful Mathematics' series for Classes 1–2 embeds mathematics in games, nature explorations, and community contexts. Maths Fest (Class 3) emphasises collaborative, festival-themed activities for multiplication, measurement, and patterns. Math Magic IV retains high activity levels while introducing fractions and data handling. The curriculum shows strong FLN alignment with explicit emphasis on making mathematics meaningful through cultural connection.
Strengths
Highest activity level outside Kerala — 'Very High' in Classes 1–2
Strong cultural contextualisation — reflects Mizo community life
Accessible language and visuals, highly suitable for tribal/indigenous learners
Weaknesses
Some topics covered less deeply than national average (e.g., geometry in Class 1)
Division coverage lighter in Class 3 compared to TN/Kerala
FLN Readiness Assessment
Strong FLN alignment — unique model of culturally embedded foundational learning. Recommended for replication in Northeast India.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Above Average

3.6 Karnataka
Board: DSERT Karnataka | Structure: Semester-based (Classes 3–4); Single book (Classes 1–2)
Karnataka's curriculum demonstrates steady progression from foundational to conceptual mathematics. Class 1 introduces shapes and numbers to 50 with activity support. The semester structure from Class 3 allows Karnataka to cover all four operations, fractions, and data handling by Class 4. The DSERT textbooks include investigative activities and problems rooted in Karnataka's geography, history, and crafts. Above Average overall; strong FLN readiness.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Above Average | FLN Score: Class 3–4: 8/10

3.7 Telangana
Board: SCERT Telangana | Structure: Single textbook per class
Telangana closely mirrors AP's curriculum structure but with greater emphasis on problem-solving in urban contexts. The SCERT Telangana textbooks include well-designed word problems with multi-step reasoning from Class 3. Fractions appear early (Class 3), data handling is explicit, and geometry is progressive. Categorized as Above Average with strong FLN alignment.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Above Average | FLN Score: Consistent 8/10

3.8 West Bengal
Board: WBBPE | Structure: Single textbook per class
West Bengal's primary mathematics curriculum (Amar Ganit, Patabhar series) shows a sound structural framework. The textbooks are more traditional in approach, with cleaner procedure-based explanations. However, Class 3–4 books (especially 'Amar Ganit') demonstrate good conceptual depth in number operations, geometry, and basic data handling. The curriculum lacks some of the exploratory activities found in Kerala/TN but is solidly Above Average for FLN.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Above Average (Classes 3–4) | FLN Score: 7–8/10

3.9 Punjab
Board: PSEB | Structure: Single textbook per class
Punjab's PSEB curriculum follows a structured, procedurally oriented approach. Content coverage is age-appropriate and complete. However, activity-based learning is limited compared to southern states. Mental mathematics is present but not systematically developed. The curriculum is solidly Average with moderate FLN alignment. Word problems use Punjab's agrarian and urban contexts well.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Average | FLN Score: 7/10

3.10 Gujarat
Board: GCERT | Structure: Single textbook per class
Gujarat's GCERT curriculum demonstrates above-average concept density for Classes 3–4. The textbooks include good activity sections and culturally relevant problems. Geometry and measurement are treated comprehensively from Class 3. The curriculum is classified as Average to Above Average with FLN scores of 7–8.
Overall Difficulty Classification: Average–Above Average | FLN Score: 7–8/10

3.11 NCERT-Following States
States adopting NCERT without significant adaptation (syllabus links available): Haryana, Jammu & Kashmir, Sikkim. Note: Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, Himachal Pradesh, Manipur, Meghalaya, Nagaland, Tripura, and Uttarakhand remain in the dataset but textbook links were unavailable; analysis for these states is based on known board affiliations and cross-reference sources only. 

The NCERT Math-Magic series (Classes 1–4) is a well-respected, FLN-aligned curriculum. Class 1 (13 chapters) covers shapes/space, numbers 1–50, addition, subtraction, time, measurement, data handling, patterns, and money. Class 2 (15 chapters) extends to 2-digit operations, weight, capacity, patterns, and data. Class 3 (14 chapters) introduces multiplication, division, fractions, geometry, measurement, and data. Class 4 (14 chapters) covers all operations, fractions, area, perimeter, geometry, and charts. The NCERT curriculum is solid but does not leverage regional/cultural contexts — a missed opportunity for contextual FLN.
Strengths: Comprehensive, child-friendly, well-illustrated, consistent national standard
Weaknesses: Generic contexts; no regional adaptation; activity level varies by chapter
Overall Difficulty Classification: Average | FLN Score: 7/10 across Classes 1–4

3.12 Remaining States
Assam / Odisha (SCERT)
Both states have developed state-specific curricula with moderate FLN alignment. Assam's curriculum incorporates Assamese cultural contexts effectively. Odisha shows stronger activity design than Bihar or MP, particularly in Odiya-medium books. FLN Score: 7/10. Classification: Average.
Madhya Pradesh
SCERT MP curriculum is structured but leans traditional. Limited activity components in Classes 1–2. Class 3–4 coverage improves. FLN Score: 6–7/10. Classification: Average.
Chhattisgarh / Rajasthan
Both states show adequate coverage with limited activity-based components. Data handling is minimal in Classes 1–2. Rajasthan has improved its Class 3–4 textbooks with better problem-solving sections. FLN Score: 6–7/10. Classification: Average.
Uttar Pradesh
UP's SCERT 'Gintara' series (Classes 3–4) shows notable improvement with good contextual problems and activity sections. Classes 1–2 follow NCERT. FLN Score: 6–7/10. Classification: Average, improving.
Bihar
Bihar's curriculum shows the weakest FLN alignment among the surveyed states. Limited activity components, heavy reliance on procedural drill, minimal data handling in Classes 1–2, and the narrowest number range (to 20 in Class 1). FLN Score: 6/10. Classification: Below Average.
Jharkhand
JAC curriculum partially follows NCERT with state adaptations. Coverage is adequate but less contextual than neighbouring Odisha or West Bengal. FLN Score: 6–7/10. Classification: Average.
4. Class-Wise Analysis
4.1 Class 1 — Foundational Stage Analysis
Key domains assessed: Number sense (1–50), Shapes/Space, Basic addition/subtraction, Measurement readiness, Patterns, Early data.
Most Advanced Curriculum: Kerala SCERT — activity-based number exploration, spatial reasoning, and early mathematical communication.
Most FLN-Aligned: Kerala (9/10), Mizoram (8/10), Maharashtra (8/10)
Fastest Concept Progression: Tamil Nadu — reaches numbers to 99 by end of Class 1; introduces pictographs and basic data.
Slowest Concept Progression: Bihar — numbers only to 20 by end of Class 1; limited topics covered.
Common Gap Observed: Mental mathematics and estimation — rarely explicitly addressed in any state's Class 1 curriculum.
Curriculum Gaps: Data handling is absent or incidental in 60% of states at Class 1; Division readiness activities largely missing.

4.2 Class 2 — Expanding Foundations
Key domains: Numbers to 100, 2-digit operations, Weight/Capacity, Patterns, Basic data handling, Introduction of place value.
Most Advanced: Tamil Nadu (to 999), Kerala (conceptual depth).
Most FLN-Aligned: Kerala (9/10), Tamil Nadu (8/10), Maharashtra (8/10), Mizoram (8/10)
Fastest Progression: Tamil Nadu extends to 3-digit numbers by end of Class 2 — a full class level ahead of most states.
Slowest Progression: Bihar — covers the least conceptual ground; stays below 100 for most of Class 2.
Common Gap: Capacity measurement is neglected in 40% of states at Class 2; Fractions not introduced in any state except Tamil Nadu at this level.

4.3 Class 3 — Building Conceptual Bridges
Key domains: Numbers to 1,000+, Multiplication, Division, Fractions, Full measurement, Data handling, Geometry.
Most Advanced: Tamil Nadu (to 9,999), Kerala (constructivist approach to all domains).
Most FLN-Aligned: Kerala (9/10), Tamil Nadu (9/10)
Strongest Problem-Solving: Tamil Nadu — dedicated 'Try This', 'Think and Do', and 'Project' sections in all chapters.
Strongest Activity-Based: Mizoram — Maths Fest design; Kerala — investigative activities throughout.
Gap: Division is explicitly taught in only 70% of states at Class 3; 30% defer to Class 4. Data handling charts appear in fewer than 50% of states' Class 3 books.

4.4 Class 4 — Preparing for Mathematical Thinking
Key domains: Large numbers (to 10,000+), All four operations, Fractions with operations, Geometry (area/perimeter), Data interpretation, Money, Time.
Most Advanced: Tamil Nadu (to 99,999; area and perimeter; complex fractions).
Most FLN-Aligned: Kerala (9/10), Tamil Nadu (9/10)
Most Comprehensive Geometry: Tamil Nadu and Kerala — cover angles, symmetry, triangles, quadrilaterals, area, perimeter.
Weakest at Class 4: Bihar — limited fraction work, basic data handling, no area/perimeter.
Gap: 'Algebraic thinking' (pattern generalisation, finding unknowns) is present only in Kerala and Tamil Nadu at Class 4; completely absent in 80% of states.

6. FLN Alignment Analysis
6.1 NIPUN Bharat Benchmarks vs State Curricula
The NIPUN Bharat mission (2021) sets specific competency benchmarks for Grade 3 completion:
Numbers: Count, read, write numbers up to 999
Addition and Subtraction: 2- and 3-digit numbers with and without regrouping
Multiplication: Multiplication tables up to 5 × 5
Division: Simple equal distribution (up to 2-digit ÷ 1-digit)
Fractions: Identify halves and quarters
Measurement: Length (cm/m), Weight (g/kg), Capacity (mL/L)
Geometry: Identify 2D and 3D shapes
Data: Read simple pictographs and bar charts

Assessment of states against NIPUN Grade 3 targets:
Exceeds NIPUN (ahead of benchmarks): Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Mizoram, AP, Telangana, Karnataka
Meets NIPUN (on target): Gujarat, West Bengal, Odisha, Assam, Punjab, UP, Jharkhand, Rajasthan, and all NCERT-following states
Partially meets NIPUN (some gaps): Bihar, Chhattisgarh, MP (in activity and data handling components)

6.2 NEP 2020 Pedagogical Alignment
NEP 2020 emphasises: play-based and activity-based learning, discovery and exploration, real-life contexts, multiple representations, inclusion, and reducing content load in favour of depth. Against these principles:
Kerala: Fully aligned — the national benchmark for NEP 2020 pedagogy at primary level
Tamil Nadu: Highly aligned — activity components strong, real-life contexts excellent
Mizoram: Highly aligned — 'Joyful Mathematics' exemplifies NEP spirit
Maharashtra, AP, Telangana, Karnataka: Well aligned — good balance
NCERT states: Moderately aligned — Math-Magic series incorporates NEP principles but real-life contexts are generic
Bihar, some MP content: Needs improvement — procedure-first without conceptual foundation

8. Key Findings
8.1 National Patterns
The majority of states (62%) are classified as 'Average' — adequate NIPUN compliance but limited innovation in pedagogy.
Activity-based learning is the single most differentiating factor between high and low FLN scores.
Data handling is the most neglected domain — absent or minimal in 45% of states at Classes 1–2.
Fractions are introduced too late in most states (Class 4); Tamil Nadu and Kerala introduce them in Class 3.
Mental mathematics is systematically developed in fewer than 30% of state curricula.
Division is introduced in Class 4 in over one-third of states — behind the NIPUN benchmark of Class 3.

8.3 Curriculum Design Trends
Semester/term structure is associated with better content pacing and depth (Kerala, TN, Mizoram, Karnataka, AP Classes 1–2)
States with dedicated SCERT textbooks consistently outperform NCERT-only adopters on FLN metrics
Cultural contextualisation is positively correlated with FLN alignment — Mizoram, Kerala, AP all use community contexts effectively
Place value is universally covered but depth varies dramatically — Kerala uses proportional reasoning while many states only use digits and positions
9. Recommendations
9.1 For State Curriculum Designers
1. Adopt Activity-First Design:
All states should redesign Classes 1–2 textbooks to lead with hands-on, contextual activities before introducing abstract notation. Kerala's model is directly replicable.
2. Introduce Data Handling by Class 2:
Simple pictographs and tally charts should be explicitly taught by end of Class 2 in all states. Tamil Nadu and AP demonstrate how to do this effectively.
3. Culturalise the NCERT Curriculum:
States following NCERT without adaptation should develop state-specific context supplements  — word problems set in local geography, festivals, and occupations.
4. Advance Fraction Introduction:
Fractions (half, quarter, third) should appear as activity-based exploration in Class 3, not as purely procedural content in Class 4.
5. Strengthen Mental Mathematics:
Every class should include a dedicated mental math section with estimation, number bonds, and mental computation strategies.
9.2 For NIPUN Bharat Implementation Teams
Use Kerala and Tamil Nadu curricula as exemplars for FLN training materials
Develop a national activity bank that can be customised by each state
Establish clear benchmarks for data handling and fractions at Class 2–3 level
Support Bihar, Chhattisgarh, and MP to update their Class 1–2 textbooks with urgency
9.3 For FLN Program Planners
Teacher training is the most critical variable — Kerala's excellent curriculum underperforms when teachers lack constructivist pedagogy skills
Assessment alignment: ensure formative assessment tools reflect FLN competencies, not just textbook exercises
Bridge materials are needed for NCERT states to close the gap with high-performing southern states
